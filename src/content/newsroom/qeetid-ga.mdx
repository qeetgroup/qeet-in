Qeet ID, the first company inside Qeet Group, is generally available today. Engineering teams can sign up and ship production sign-in the same afternoon. The product, documentation, and pricing live at [id.qeet.in](https://id.qeet.in).

We started Qeet ID from a question we kept hearing inside other companies: why does every engineering team still have to choose between developer-friendly auth and enterprise-grade auth? The two have been treated as separate categories of product for fifteen years, and the result is that teams either outgrow their tooling at the worst possible moment or pay enterprise prices long before they need enterprise features. We do not believe that trade is permanent. Qeet ID is what closes it.

The capability surface is what modern teams actually need from identity, in one platform. Email, passwordless magic-link, and one-time-code sign-in. Multi-factor with authenticator apps and single-use recovery codes. Multi-tenant role-based access control behind a single permission-check API. Machine-to-machine auth over the OAuth 2.0 client-credentials grant. Standards-based OpenID Connect. Stateful, revocable sessions, multi-tenant isolation, and a hash-chained audit log. A standards-based API any stack can integrate against, with first-party SDKs on the way.

The free tier covers five thousand monthly active users with no credit card. The paid tiers are documented on the site. The platform is built GDPR-aligned from day one — tenant isolation, revocable sessions, and an append-only, hash-chained audit log are part of the architecture, not bolted on later. SOC 2 and a public trust center are on the roadmap as the product hardens toward 1.0.

There is more to do. There will be more integrations, the first SDKs, more depth in the platform&rsquo;s edges. The point of generally available is not "the product is finished" — it is "the product is ready for the people who will tell us what to build next." If you are running auth at a team that is growing fast, we&rsquo;d like to hear what you find.
